# Loss Analysis: Gannett Digital — $680,000

## Deal Summary

Gannett Digital (USA TODAY Network) evaluated our platform for yield optimization and audience data activation across their network of 200+ local news properties. The deal would have been our largest single-publisher contract. After a 127-day sales cycle including a successful POC, Gannett chose to expand their Google Ad Manager deployment and build audience targeting capabilities in-house.

## What We Competed Against

- **Google Ad Manager (GAM) expansion:** Incumbent ad server with deep integration. Google offered expanded features + pricing concession to retain the account.
- **In-house build:** Gannett's engineering team proposed building audience segmentation on top of their existing Snowflake data warehouse.
- **TradeDesk:** Evaluated briefly for demand-side capabilities but dropped early.

## Why We Lost

### Stated Reason (from the buyer)
"We've decided to invest in our own data capabilities. With our engineering resources, we can build what we need on top of GAM and Snowflake without adding another vendor."

### Actual Reason (our assessment)
Three factors, in order of importance:

1. **CTO preference for internal build.** Gannett's CTO (our blocker) had a philosophical bias toward owning technology rather than licensing it. He valued engineering control over speed-to-market. We never won him over.

2. **Google's counter-move.** When Google learned Gannett was evaluating alternatives, they offered a 30% discount on GAM, free implementation of Google's audience tools, and a dedicated support team for 12 months. This was a retention play that dramatically changed the competitive dynamics.

3. **Champion lost influence.** Our champion (VP of Digital Advertising) was strong early in the process but lost political ground to the CTO during Q3 planning. By the time the decision was made, the CTO's voice carried more weight than our champion's.

## Turning Point

**Week 10 — Google counter-offer.** Up until week 10, we were the frontrunner. Our POC showed 22% yield improvement on pilot sites, and the VP of Digital Advertising was building the internal business case. Then Google's strategic accounts team flew to Gannett's headquarters with a retention package. The conversation shifted from "which vendor optimizes yield best?" to "do we even need a new vendor if Google expands their offering?"

We didn't have a response prepared for this scenario. By the time we adjusted our messaging, the CTO had already framed the decision as "build vs. buy" rather than "our platform vs. theirs."

## Key Lessons

1. **Always plan for the incumbent counter-move.** In any displacement deal, assume the incumbent will offer concessions. Prepare the buyer for this: "When [incumbent] hears you're evaluating alternatives, they'll offer you a deal. Here's what to watch out for..."

2. **Identify and neutralize blockers early.** The CTO was skeptical from meeting 1, and we treated him as a non-factor because our champion was strong. When the power dynamic shifted, we had no relationship with the person who mattered most.

3. **"Build vs. buy" is the hardest competitor to beat.** When the buyer's engineering team wants to build, logic alone won't win. You need to quantify the TOTAL cost of building (engineering time, maintenance, opportunity cost of engineers not working on core product) and create urgency around time-to-market.

4. **Champion health is a leading indicator.** Our champion's responsiveness declined over weeks 8-12, and we didn't escalate. By the time we noticed the problem, the decision was already made.

5. **POC success doesn't guarantee a win.** We proved the value quantitatively. But value is necessary, not sufficient — when political dynamics shift, even great POC results get discounted.

## What We Would Do Differently

1. Build a direct relationship with the CTO from week 1, not week 10
2. Pre-brief the champion on the inevitable Google counter-offer
3. Include a "total cost of in-house build" analysis in our proposal proactively
4. Monitor champion engagement as a health metric — when responsiveness drops, escalate immediately
5. Engage the COO (economic buyer) more directly in the middle of the cycle, not just at the beginning

## Pattern Recognition

This is the second deal we've lost to "in-house build" in 2025 (the first was Hearst Digital at $340K). Pattern: large publishers with 50+ engineers and a CTO who prefers control over speed. **We should consider qualifying out of deals where the CTO has an explicit "build first" philosophy, or pre-positioning with a total cost of ownership analysis before the build option is raised.**
