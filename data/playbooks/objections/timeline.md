# Timeline Objection Playbook

## Common Forms

- "Not right now"
- "Maybe next quarter"
- "We're not ready yet"
- "We need to focus on other priorities first"
- "Let's revisit in Q3"
- "We're in the middle of [other initiative]"
- "The timing isn't right"

## Core Principle

Timeline objections are almost never about timing — they're about priority. "Not now" means "not important enough to act on now." Your job is to either increase urgency (make inaction feel costly) or reduce friction (make action feel easy). If you can't do either, the deal isn't real.

---

## Response 1: Cost of Delay

**When to use:** The buyer has acknowledged real pain but doesn't feel urgency. They're comfortable with the status quo despite its costs.

**The response:**

"I completely understand — your plate is full, and adding another initiative isn't a small decision. Let me share one data point that might affect the calculus. You mentioned that [specific pain] is costing approximately $[X] per [month/quarter]. If we start in [current month], you'd see results by [month + 90 days], meaning you'd recover roughly $[savings] by year-end. If we wait until [their suggested timeline], that's [N months] of continued cost — approximately $[total delay cost] — before you even start recovering.

I'm not trying to create false urgency. But there's a real cost to waiting, and I want to make sure that cost is factored into your timing decision. Does that change how you're thinking about the timeline?"

**Why it works:** It makes the implicit cost of waiting explicit. Most buyers haven't calculated what delay costs them. When they see a specific number, "later" stops feeling free.

**Media sales context:** "Your CPAs are rising at 34% annually. Every month you wait, you're spending approximately $12,500 more than you need to for the same results. By Q3, that's $50K in avoidable waste. If we start now, we can have your campaigns optimized before your Q3 campaign flights — which means Q3 performance improves instead of continuing to decline."

---

## Response 2: Competitive Window

**When to use:** The buyer's competitors are already investing in similar solutions, or there's a market window that closes.

**The response:**

"That makes sense. I want to flag one thing I'm seeing in the market, though — and you can decide how much weight to give it. [Competitor peer 1] and [competitor peer 2] have both invested in [similar capability] in the last 12 months. They're already pitching advertisers with [specific capability your solution enables]. Every quarter that passes, they build more data, more case studies, and more advertiser confidence in their offering. The gap doesn't stay constant — it widens.

I'm not saying you need to rush into this. But I want you to have the full picture when you decide when to move. Is there a way to start smaller now — even a pilot — so you're building capability while you finalize your broader priorities?"

**Why it works:** Loss aversion is a stronger motivator than gain. The fear of falling behind competitors creates urgency that "you'll save money" doesn't.

---

## Response 3: Pilot Bridge

**When to use:** The buyer genuinely can't commit to a full implementation now, but you want to maintain momentum and get a foothold.

**The response:**

"I hear you — a full implementation may not be realistic this quarter given everything else on your plate. What if we took a different approach? Instead of the full scope, let's run a 60-day pilot focused on [their most urgent pain point]. Here's what that looks like:

- **Scope:** [Specific, narrow scope — one campaign, one channel, one audience segment]
- **Investment:** $[smaller amount — typically 15-25% of full deal]
- **Success metric:** [Specific, measurable outcome they care about]
- **Timeline:** Results within 60 days, review meeting on day 45
- **Decision point:** If the pilot delivers [metric], we expand to the full implementation. If not, you walk away with data and no further obligation.

This way, you're not adding a major initiative to your Q[current] plate. You're running a low-risk test that gives you the data to make a confident decision for Q[next]. And your team doesn't need to shift priorities — we handle 90% of the work.

Does a pilot structure make sense given where you are right now?"

**Why it works:** It removes the three barriers that cause timeline objections: commitment size (smaller), risk (defined exit), and effort (you do the work). Most pilots convert to full deals because once the buyer sees results, the "timing" concern disappears.

---

## Response 4: Calendar Anchor

**When to use:** There's a natural business event that creates a real deadline — upfront season, fiscal year planning, a product launch, a contract expiration.

**The response:**

"I want to make sure we're aligned on calendar dynamics. You mentioned that [specific event — upfront negotiations start in May / your fiscal year resets in July / your current vendor contract expires in September]. Working backwards from that date:

- If the contract needs to be signed by [date], legal/procurement needs [X weeks], which means...
- The proposal needs to be approved by [date], which means...
- We need to complete evaluation by [date], which means...
- Our next meeting should be [date]

If we don't start the process by [specific date], we miss the [event] window, and you're either locked into the status quo for another [cycle length] or you're rushing the evaluation at the last minute. Neither of those is great for you.

I'm not trying to create pressure — I'm trying to help you avoid a crunch later. Does this reverse timeline make sense? Can we lock in [next meeting date] to keep the option open?"

**Why it works:** It connects the abstract "later" to a concrete calendar reality. When the buyer sees the reverse timeline, "later" often means "too late," which converts the timeline objection into urgency.

**Media sales context:** "Upfront season starts in earnest in May. If you want to pitch advertisers a data-driven targeting capability for the next upfront cycle, the platform needs to be live and tested by April. Working backwards, that means contract by February, integration by March, first test campaigns in April. We're already in [current month] — the window is tight but doable if we move this month."

## Diagnosis: Real Timing Issue vs. Polite Brush-Off

| Signal | Real Timing Issue | Polite Brush-Off |
|---|---|---|
| They suggest a specific future date | Real | — |
| They say "let's revisit" without a date | — | Brush-off |
| They explain the competing priority in detail | Real | — |
| They're vague about what changes later | — | Brush-off |
| They're willing to schedule a follow-up now | Real | — |
| They say "I'll reach out when we're ready" | — | Brush-off |
| They ask you to send materials for later review | — | Brush-off |
| They involve other stakeholders in the timeline discussion | Real | — |

**If it's real:** Use Pilot Bridge or Calendar Anchor. Maintain the relationship and create a concrete path forward.

**If it's a brush-off:** Go back to Isolate (AIRC). The real objection isn't timing — it's something else. Ask: "I want to be respectful of your time. Is the timing the main thing, or is there something about the solution itself that isn't clicking?"
