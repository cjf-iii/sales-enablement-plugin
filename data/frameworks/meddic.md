# MEDDIC Framework — Complete Reference

## Overview

MEDDIC is the industry-standard enterprise sales qualification framework. Developed at PTC in the 1990s by Jack Napoli and Dick Dunkel, it has since become the most widely adopted qualification methodology for complex B2B sales. MEDDIC forces rigor into deal qualification by requiring evidence — not assumptions — for each dimension.

**When to use MEDDIC:** Any deal with a value above $50K, a sales cycle longer than 30 days, or more than 2 stakeholders involved in the decision.

**Core principle:** A deal is only as strong as its weakest MEDDIC dimension. A perfect score on 5 dimensions and a 2 on one dimension means the deal is at risk.

## The Six Dimensions

### M — Metrics

**Definition:** The quantified business outcomes the buyer expects from your solution. Not features, not capabilities — measurable results tied to their P&L, KPIs, or strategic objectives.

**Why it matters:** Metrics transform your solution from a "want" to a "need." Without quantified value, the economic buyer has no basis for approving the investment. Metrics also become the standard against which your solution is measured post-sale — setting the right ones prevents churn.

**What good looks like:**
- The buyer can articulate specific targets: "We need to reduce CPA from $42 to $28"
- The metrics tie to a strategic initiative: "This supports our board mandate to grow digital revenue 40%"
- The buyer has validated your ROI model with their own data
- The buyer is using your metrics in internal conversations

**What bad looks like:**
- "They want better performance" (vague, unmeasurable)
- You built the ROI model but the buyer hasn't confirmed the inputs
- The metrics are yours, not theirs — you're projecting, they're not committed

**Discovery questions:**
1. "What does success look like in numbers 12 months after launch?"
2. "What KPIs are you measured on personally? How does this initiative move those?"
3. "What's the cost of the status quo for another quarter?"
4. "If this works perfectly, what number changes on your dashboard?"

**Scoring guide:** See the detailed 1-10 rubric in the deal-analysis skill.

### E — Economic Buyer

**Definition:** The individual who has the authority to approve the expenditure and allocate budget. Not the person who evaluates the solution, not the person who recommends — the person who says "yes, spend the money."

**Why it matters:** No matter how well-qualified the rest of the deal is, without economic buyer engagement, the deal cannot close. Deals without EB access close at less than 15% — and the ones that do close take 2-3x longer because the EB introduces surprise requirements late.

**Identifying the EB:**
- They can approve budget without further escalation (for this deal size)
- Their title often includes VP, SVP, C-level, or GM
- They care about business outcomes, not product features
- They ask about risk, ROI, and strategic alignment — not about UI or integrations
- Your champion defers to them on budget questions

**Common traps:**
- "My boss will approve whatever I recommend" → Almost never true for deals above $50K
- A committee with no clear decision-maker → Find who has veto power
- The EB delegates evaluation but retains approval → You still need their buy-in

**Discovery questions:**
1. "Walk me through your approval process for an investment of this size"
2. "Who ultimately signs off on the budget? Will they be part of our conversations?"
3. "Have you secured budget for this initiative, or is that part of the process?"
4. "What would [EB name] need to see to feel confident about this investment?"

### D — Decision Criteria

**Definition:** The specific criteria the buyer will use to evaluate and compare solutions. This includes both explicit criteria (RFP requirements, scorecard items) and implicit criteria (political preferences, risk tolerance, brand bias).

**Why it matters:** If you don't know the criteria, you can't position against them. If you know them but didn't influence them, you're playing someone else's game. The best deals are ones where you helped shape the evaluation criteria so that they naturally favor your strengths.

**Levels of influence:**
1. **Unaware:** You don't know how they'll decide → dangerous
2. **Aware:** You know their criteria but didn't shape them → reactive
3. **Influential:** You suggested criteria that they adopted → advantaged
4. **Dominant:** The criteria were co-created with you → strongly positioned

**How to influence criteria:**
- Share "best practices" for evaluating solutions in your category
- Suggest criteria based on what your best customers found important
- Propose a joint evaluation framework early in the process
- Introduce criteria that are unique to your strengths (without being obvious)

**Discovery questions:**
1. "How will you evaluate the solutions you're considering? Is there a formal process?"
2. "Beyond the technical requirements, what else matters to the decision-makers?"
3. "What criteria did you use last time you made a similar purchase? What would you change?"
4. "Which of these criteria are must-haves versus nice-to-haves?"

### D — Decision Process

**Definition:** The step-by-step process the buyer will follow from evaluation through signed contract. This includes: who's involved at each step, what deliverables are required, what approvals are needed, and what the timeline is.

**Why it matters:** Deals don't stall because buyers say no — they stall because the process is undefined. Without a mapped decision process, you can't forecast accurately, you can't anticipate blockers, and you can't build a mutual action plan.

**What a mapped process looks like:**
1. Technical evaluation (2 weeks) → Led by [name]
2. Business case presentation to leadership (1 meeting) → Led by champion
3. Procurement/legal review (2-3 weeks) → Led by [procurement contact]
4. Final approval by EB → Led by [EB name]
5. Contract signature → Target date: [date]

**Common hidden steps:**
- Security review (adds 2-4 weeks in regulated industries)
- Board approval (for deals above certain thresholds)
- Budget reallocation request (if no pre-approved budget exists)
- Reference checks (the buyer talks to your customers without telling you)
- Internal competing initiatives (your deal competes with other projects for the same budget)

**Discovery questions:**
1. "Between now and a signed contract, what steps need to happen?"
2. "Have you bought a solution like this before? What did that process look like?"
3. "Are there any approval steps or reviews that could add time to the timeline?"
4. "If we agreed on a solution today, when would you realistically be able to start?"

### I — Identify Pain

**Definition:** The specific business problem that your solution solves — not the feature they want, but the PAIN that's costing them money, time, competitive position, or personal career risk.

**Why it matters:** Pain is the engine that drives deals forward. Without pain, there's no urgency. Without urgency, deals die of indifference. The depth of pain you've identified directly correlates with close rate and deal velocity.

**Pain hierarchy (from weakest to strongest):**
1. **Latent pain:** They have a problem but don't know it yet → requires education
2. **Acknowledged pain:** They know the problem exists but aren't actively solving it → requires urgency
3. **Active pain:** They're actively looking for a solution → qualified opportunity
4. **Critical pain:** There's a deadline, mandate, or crisis forcing action → high urgency, accelerated timeline

**Pain vs. Symptoms:**
- Symptom: "Our CPA is too high"
- Pain: "Rising CPA is eroding our unit economics, and if we don't fix it by Q3, we miss our annual targets and I lose my performance bonus"
- The symptom is what they tell you first. The pain is what you uncover by asking "why" and "so what" multiple times.

**Discovery questions:**
1. "What's the business impact of this problem? Can you put a number on it?"
2. "How long has this been an issue? Why solve it now versus 6 months ago?"
3. "What happens if you don't solve this in the next quarter?"
4. "Who else in the organization feels this pain? How does it affect their work?"
5. "What have you tried so far? What worked and what didn't?"

---

### C — Champion

**Definition:** An internal advocate who has POWER (organizational influence), ACCESS (to the economic buyer and other stakeholders), and MOTIVATION (personal stake in your solution winning).

**Why it matters:** You cannot sell from the outside. A champion sells for you when you're not in the room. They navigate internal politics, coach you on objections, and provide intelligence about the competitive landscape and decision dynamics.

**Champion vs. Coach vs. Supporter:**
- **Supporter:** Likes you, takes your calls, shares information → but has no power to influence the decision
- **Coach:** Gives you insider information and advice → but may not actively advocate for you
- **Champion:** Actively sells on your behalf internally, has credibility with the EB, and stakes their reputation on the outcome

**Champion test (all three must be true):**
1. **Will they give you access** to people and information you couldn't get on your own?
2. **Will they sell for you** in meetings where you're not present?
3. **Do they have something to gain** personally from your solution winning? (Career advancement, problem solved, recognition)

**How to develop a champion:**
- Help them understand what they personally gain from your solution winning
- Equip them with talking points and materials for internal selling
- Coach them on anticipated objections and how to handle them
- Make them look good to their leadership by providing strategic insights
- Never go around them — always through them (unless they ask you to)

**Discovery questions:**
1. "Who on your team cares most about solving this problem?"
2. "If you were to recommend our solution, what would your boss ask you?"
3. "How can I help you make the case internally?"
4. "What would make you confident enough to present this to [EB]?"

## MEDDIC Scoring Summary Table

| Dimension | Weight | Score 1-3 | Score 4-6 | Score 7-8 | Score 9-10 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Metrics | 15% | No metrics identified | Vague metrics, not validated | Specific, buyer-confirmed metrics | Buyer-authored, presented to leadership |
| Economic Buyer | 20% | Unknown or unengaged | Identified, minimal contact | Direct engagement, budget confirmed | Actively driving the deal |
| Decision Criteria | 15% | Unknown | Known but not influenced | Known and partially influenced | Co-authored with buyer |
| Decision Process | 15% | Unknown | General sense, no timeline | Mapped with dates | Joint execution plan with milestones |
| Identify Pain | 20% | No pain identified | Surface-level pain | Quantified, multi-stakeholder pain | Urgent with compelling event |
| Champion | 15% | No advocate | Friendly but powerless | Active advocate with influence | Reputation staked, selling internally |

## Overall Score Interpretation

| Score Range | Assessment | Forecast Inclusion |
|---|---|---|
| 0-15 | Unqualified — needs significant work or disqualification | Do not forecast |
| 16-30 | Early qualification — promising but major gaps remain | Pipeline only, not forecast |
| 31-42 | Qualified — real opportunity with manageable gaps | Include in upside forecast |
| 43-52 | Well-qualified — strong deal with minor gaps | Include in commit forecast |
| 53-60 | Exceptional — all dimensions validated with evidence | High-confidence commit |
