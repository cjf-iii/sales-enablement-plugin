# Price Objection Playbook

## Common Forms

- "It's too expensive"
- "We don't have the budget"
- "Your competitor is cheaper"
- "Can you do it for less?"
- "We need to reduce the scope to fit our budget"
- "Our agency says we can get this cheaper"
- "The CPMs are too high"

## Core Principle

Never defend your price. Instead, make the buyer defend the cost of NOT buying. Price is only "too high" relative to perceived value. If the buyer says your price is too high, you have a value problem, not a price problem.

---

## Response 1: The ROI Reframe

**When to use:** The buyer focuses on the absolute number without connecting it to the return. Best for buyers who haven't seen a financial model.

**The response:**

"I completely understand — $[X] is a significant investment and you're right to scrutinize it. Let me flip the lens for a moment. You mentioned that [specific pain] is costing you approximately $[Y] per [time period]. That means doing nothing for another 6 months costs $[Y/2]. Our solution, based on what we've seen with companies like yours, typically delivers [specific outcome] within [timeline], which translates to $[Z] in [savings/revenue]. So the question isn't whether $[X] is a lot of money — it is. The question is whether $[X] to solve a $[Y] problem is a good trade. Does that math change how you're thinking about the investment?"

**Why it works:** It reframes the conversation from "cost" to "return on investment." The buyer stops comparing your price to zero and starts comparing it to the cost of their problem.

**Media sales context:** "Your CPAs have risen 34% YoY — that's roughly $150K in additional spend for the same acquisition volume. Our annual investment is $350K, but at a 25% CPA reduction, you recover $220K in year one. Net cost: $130K. Net customer acquisition increase: 5,400. That's $24 per incremental customer. How does that compare to your other acquisition channels?"

## Response 2: Scope Alignment

**When to use:** The buyer has a real budget constraint (not a negotiation tactic) and you need to find a path forward that works financially.

**The response:**

"I hear you, and I don't want to put you in a difficult position with your budget. Let me suggest something: instead of reducing our price — which would mean reducing what we deliver — let's align the scope to your most critical priorities. You mentioned [pain point 1] and [pain point 2] are the top priorities. What if we structured a Phase 1 that focuses exclusively on those two areas at $[reduced amount], with a clear success metric? Once we've proven the value in Phase 1, we can expand to the full scope in Phase 2 with the data to justify the investment internally. Would that approach work with your budget cycle?"

**Why it works:** It protects your pricing integrity while giving the buyer a real path forward. It also creates a land-and-expand motion that often leads to larger total deal value than the original proposal.

**Media sales context:** "Rather than a full 12-month, multi-platform campaign at $480K, what if we start with a 6-month programmatic optimization engagement at $200K? We focus entirely on reducing your CPA — which you said is the most urgent issue. If we hit the 25% CPA reduction target, we'll have the data to justify the full integrated campaign for next year's upfront. Does a phased approach like that align with how your budget works?"

## Response 3: Risk Reversal

**When to use:** The buyer's price concern is really about risk — they're afraid of spending the money and not getting results. Common with first-time buyers or buyers who've been burned before.

**The response:**

"What I'm hearing is less about the number itself and more about confidence that you'll see the return. Is that fair? Because if it is, let me remove that risk. We're willing to structure this with [specific risk reversal: performance guarantee / pilot period / success-based milestone payments / exit clause]. Here's what that would look like: we start with a [pilot/proof period] at [reduced commitment]. We agree on [specific success metrics] upfront. If we don't hit them, [consequence — you can walk away / we refund the difference / we continue at no charge until we do]. That way, you're not paying $[X] on hope — you're paying based on demonstrated results. Would that structure make you comfortable moving forward?"

**Why it works:** It addresses the real objection (risk, not price) and demonstrates confidence in your solution. Buyers who are offered a guarantee rarely exercise it — but the option to do so removes the fear that drives price objections.

---

## Response 4: Competitive TCO (Total Cost of Ownership)

**When to use:** The buyer is comparing your price to a cheaper competitor and using the competitor's price as leverage.

**The response:**

"I appreciate the transparency — and you should absolutely compare options. Let me help you do an apples-to-apples comparison, because sticker price can be misleading. [Competitor] charges $[their price] for the platform, but let me walk through the total cost of ownership:

Their platform fee is $[X]. But you'll also need:
- [Hidden cost 1]: $[amount] — [explanation, e.g., 'data segment costs that are included in our price']
- [Hidden cost 2]: $[amount] — [explanation, e.g., '1-2 FTEs to operate the platform at $80-120K each']
- [Hidden cost 3]: $[amount] — [explanation, e.g., 'integration and setup costs']

Total cost of ownership with [competitor]: $[total]. Our all-in price: $[your price].

And that's before we compare performance. Their average [key metric] for companies your size is [X]. Ours is [Y]. Over 12 months, that performance gap represents approximately $[difference] in [revenue/savings].

I'm not saying they're a bad option — but I want to make sure you're comparing the full picture, not just the first number on the page."

**Why it works:** Most buyers haven't done a TCO analysis. When you do it for them, you demonstrate expertise, build trust, and often reveal that the "cheaper" option isn't actually cheaper.

---

## Response 5: Executive Value Story

**When to use:** The economic buyer needs a peer-level example to justify the investment. Best deployed after the champion has done initial internal selling and needs reinforcement.

**The response:**

"Let me share a quick story that might be relevant. [Similar company] — similar size, similar industry, facing the same [specific challenge] you're dealing with. Their VP of Marketing had the same reaction to our price: 'That's more than we planned to spend.' But she ran the numbers with her CFO and realized that their current approach was costing them $[X] per quarter in [wasted spend / lost revenue / operational inefficiency]. They signed with us at $[similar price point]. Within [timeframe], they saw [specific result]: [metric 1 improved by X%], [metric 2 improved by Y%], and their CFO told us — and I quote — '[direct quote about value received].'

I'd love to connect you with [reference name] if a conversation with a peer would be helpful. She's offered to take calls from companies evaluating us because she wishes someone had told her how much the delay was costing her."

**Why it works:** Social proof from a peer is more persuasive than any argument you can make. Offering a reference call shows confidence and gives the buyer a safe way to validate the investment.

## Diagnosis: Is This a Real Objection or a Tactic?

| Signal | Likely Real | Likely Tactic |
|---|---|---|
| They share their actual budget number | Real | — |
| They say "too expensive" without asking about value | — | Tactic |
| They mention competing priorities for the same budget | Real | — |
| They bring up competitor pricing unprompted | — | Tactic |
| They ask for a discount as the very first response | — | Tactic |
| They express genuine concern about justifying it internally | Real | — |
| They've gone through deep discovery and still push on price | Real | — |
| They push on price before discovery is complete | — | Tactic or shopping |

**If it's a tactic:** Hold firm. Respond with ROI Reframe or TCO. They'll buy either way — they're testing your resolve.

**If it's real:** Offer Scope Alignment or Risk Reversal. Find a path that works without discounting.
