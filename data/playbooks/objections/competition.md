# Competition Objection Playbook

## Common Forms

- "We're also looking at [competitor]"
- "Why should we choose you over [competitor]?"
- "[Competitor] can do everything you do for less"
- "We already use [incumbent] and they're fine"
- "Our agency recommended [competitor]"
- "Can you match what [competitor] is offering?"

## Core Principle

Never trash the competitor. It makes you look insecure and the buyer trusts you less. Instead, differentiate on FIT — how your solution specifically matches THEIR situation better than the alternative. Acknowledge the competitor's strengths, then redirect to the dimensions where you win.

---

## Response 1: Unique Fit

**When to use:** The buyer asks a general "why you vs. them" question. This is your chance to reframe the evaluation around criteria that favor you.

**The response:**

"That's a smart evaluation — [competitor] is a solid company, and I'd be surprised if you weren't looking at them. The honest answer is that [competitor] is a great fit for some companies and we're a great fit for others. The question is which one fits YOUR situation.

Here's what I'd focus on for your specific needs: You told me your top priorities are [priority 1] and [priority 2]. On [priority 1], here's how we're different: [specific differentiation with evidence]. On [priority 2], here's what makes our approach unique: [specific differentiation with evidence].

Where [competitor] tends to be stronger is [honest acknowledgment of their advantage]. For some companies, that matters a lot. For your situation — where [buyer's specific context] — I think [our advantage] is more impactful. But I'd rather show you than tell you. Can I walk you through [specific proof — a demo, a case study, a reference call] so you can judge for yourself?"

**Why it works:** Honesty about the competitor builds trust. Specificity about their situation shows preparation. Focusing on fit instead of features reframes the evaluation in your favor.

---

## Response 2: Trap Questions

**When to use:** You know the competitor's specific weaknesses and want the buyer to discover them on their own.

**The response:**

"Great — thorough evaluations lead to better decisions. Let me suggest a few questions you should ask every vendor you're evaluating, including us. These are the questions that separate marketing claims from operational reality:

1. '[Trap question 1 — targets competitor weakness]' — This matters because [reason tied to buyer's situation].
2. '[Trap question 2 — targets competitor weakness]' — Ask them to show you this live, not on a slide.
3. '[Trap question 3 — targets competitor weakness]' — Get specific numbers, not ranges.

We're happy to answer these same questions — and I'll give you specific, verifiable answers. The vendor who gives you vague responses to these questions is the one hiding something."

**Why it works:** You're not attacking the competitor — you're helping the buyer be a better evaluator. The questions are designed to expose weaknesses the buyer wouldn't discover without guidance. Offering to answer the same questions yourself demonstrates confidence.

---

## Response 3: Risk Contrast

**When to use:** The competitor has known reliability, support, or implementation issues. Best when supported by specific evidence.

**The response:**

"I want to raise something that doesn't always come up in sales evaluations but matters a lot post-sale: operational risk. When you're evaluating solutions, there are two questions: 'Does it do what I need?' and 'Can I rely on it when it matters?'

On the first question, [competitor] and we probably look similar on a feature matrix. On the second question, I'd encourage you to dig into [specific risk area — e.g., their SLA track record, their support model, their implementation timeline, their customer churn]. Specifically, ask them: '[pointed question about their risk area].'

Here's our track record on that same dimension: [specific data — uptime %, support response time, implementation success rate, retention rate]. And here's a customer who can speak to this directly: [reference name] evaluated both options and chose us specifically because of [risk-related differentiator].

I'm not saying [competitor] won't deliver. I'm saying you should verify — because switching costs are real, and the cheapest vendor on day one isn't always the cheapest over the life of the contract."

**Why it works:** It shifts evaluation from "which features do I get?" to "which vendor can I trust?" This is powerful because risk aversion is a stronger motivator than feature desire, especially for senior decision-makers.

---

## Response 4: Proof Points

**When to use:** The buyer is leaning toward a competitor and needs a compelling reason to reconsider. Deploy your strongest social proof.

**The response:**

"I appreciate your openness about the evaluation. Let me share something directly relevant: [reference company] was in almost exactly your position last year. They were evaluating [competitor] and us for [same use case]. Here's what happened:

They initially leaned toward [competitor] because of [the same reason the buyer is leaning that way]. But when they dug deeper, they found [specific discovery — higher TCO, weaker performance, implementation challenges]. They chose us, and within [timeframe], they saw [specific results].

[Reference contact name], their [title], has offered to take calls from companies going through the same evaluation. Would a 15-minute conversation with someone who's been exactly where you are be helpful? I think they'd give you an honest perspective — including what they wish we did better."

**Why it works:** Nothing is more persuasive than a peer who made the same decision and can share first-hand experience. Offering to share "what we could do better" signals honesty and makes the reference feel unscripted.

---

## Handling "We Already Use [Incumbent]"

This is the hardest competitive scenario. The incumbent has relationships, integration, and switching costs on their side.

**Strategy: Don't try to replace everything. Win a wedge.**

"I'm not going to suggest you rip out [incumbent] — that would be disruptive and risky, and I wouldn't recommend it. What I'd suggest is this: most of our customers who currently use [incumbent] started by running us alongside them for [specific use case — one channel, one campaign type, one audience segment]. No disruption to their current operations. Within [timeline], they had a direct, head-to-head comparison on actual performance data — not vendor claims.

In 7 out of 10 cases, the head-to-head data led to an expanded partnership with us. In the other 3, they stayed with [incumbent] and at least had confidence they were with the right partner. Either way, you win — you get better data to make a better decision. Would a side-by-side pilot make sense for your team?"

---

## Diagnosis: Competitive Pressure or Leverage?

| Signal | Real Evaluation | Using Competitor for Leverage |
|---|---|---|
| They share specific details about the competitor's offering | Real | — |
| They mention the competitor's price without being asked | — | Leverage |
| They've had multiple meetings with the competitor | Real | — |
| They name-drop the competitor once and don't elaborate | — | Leverage |
| They ask you to match specific competitor features | Real | — |
| They ask for a discount "because [competitor] is cheaper" | — | Leverage |

**If real:** Execute full competitive strategy — trap questions, proof points, differentiation.

**If leverage:** Acknowledge, then redirect to value. "I'm glad you have options. Let me make sure you're comparing the full picture, not just the price tag."
