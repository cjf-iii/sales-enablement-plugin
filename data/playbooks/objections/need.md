# Need Objection Playbook

## Common Forms

- "We don't really need this right now"
- "What we have works fine"
- "We're not looking to make changes"
- "I don't see how this applies to us"
- "We've got bigger priorities"
- "This is a nice-to-have, not a must-have"

## Core Principle

If the buyer says they don't need your solution, one of three things is true: (1) you haven't found the right pain yet, (2) they genuinely don't have the problem you solve, or (3) they have the problem but haven't felt its full impact. Responses 1 and 3 are solvable. Response 2 means you should qualify out gracefully.

---

## Response 1: Impact Questions (Deepen Discovery)

**When to use:** You suspect the buyer has the problem but hasn't connected it to business impact. They've normalized the pain.

**The response:**

"I hear you — and if your current approach is working, the last thing I'd want is to fix something that isn't broken. But let me ask a few questions, because sometimes the biggest costs are the ones we've gotten used to paying:

1. 'When you [specific process related to their workflow], how long does that take your team? And how often does it happen?'
2. 'What happens when [specific failure scenario related to your solution area]? How does the team handle it?'
3. 'If I asked your [colleagues in a specific role — ad ops, marketing analysts, campaign managers] what they'd fix if they had a magic wand, what do you think they'd say?'

I'm not trying to convince you that you have a problem you don't have. I just want to make sure we're looking at the full picture before we both decide this isn't a fit."

**Why it works:** Most need objections dissolve when you ask the right questions. The buyer has normalized their pain — they've been living with it so long that it doesn't register as a problem. Impact questions make the invisible visible.

**Media sales example:** "You mentioned your campaign setup takes about 3 weeks. How many campaigns do you launch per month? So roughly 12 weeks of team time per month on setup alone? At your team's loaded cost, that's about $[X] per month in operational overhead. What would your team do with those hours if the setup were automated?"

---

## Response 2: Peer Pressure (Competitive Context)

**When to use:** The buyer is complacent about a problem that their industry peers are actively solving. The risk isn't internal — it's competitive.

**The response:**

"I respect that — you know your business better than I do. I will say that I'm having this conversation with a lot of companies in [their industry] right now, and there's a pattern emerging. Companies like [peer 1] and [peer 2] are investing in [capability your solution provides] because they're seeing [specific trend — e.g., advertiser demand for data-driven targeting, rising CPAs, audience fragmentation].

The companies making this investment now are building a capability moat — they're accumulating data, refining their models, and strengthening their advertiser pitch every quarter. Companies that wait aren't standing still; they're falling behind relative to the ones moving forward.

I don't want to create artificial urgency. But I do want you to have the full market context. Would it be valuable to see what [peer company] is doing differently and the results they're seeing? Even if you decide not to act now, it's useful intelligence for your strategic planning."

**Why it works:** People are more afraid of falling behind peers than they are motivated by abstract improvements. Competitive framing transforms "nice to have" into "strategic imperative." And offering the information as intelligence rather than a sales pitch keeps the door open.

---

## Response 3: Future State (Vision Gap)

**When to use:** The buyer is satisfied with their current state but hasn't considered where they need to be in 12-18 months.

**The response:**

"That's fair — today, your current setup is handling the basics. Let me ask a forward-looking question: Where does [their company] need to be in 18 months? What are you building toward?

[Pause and listen]

Okay, so you're aiming for [their stated goal — e.g., 50% digital revenue, premium direct-sold tier, data-driven ad targeting]. Let me map that against your current capabilities:

- To get to [goal], you'd need [capability 1]. Do you have that today? [Usually no]
- You'd also need [capability 2]. What's your plan to build that? [Usually unclear]
- And [capability 3] — which is table stakes for [their goal] based on what we see across the industry.

The gap between where you are and where you're going isn't small, and it takes [timeframe] to build this capability. If you start today, you're ready by [date]. If you start in [their suggested later date], you're ready by [much later date] — and by then, [competitive consequence].

I'm not suggesting you need everything today. But if [goal] is real, the building needs to start now. What would a roadmap conversation look like for you?"

**Why it works:** It shifts the frame from "do I have a problem today?" to "am I building toward where I need to be?" Most buyers are future-oriented thinkers — connecting your solution to their vision is more compelling than connecting it to their current pain.

---

## Qualifying Out Gracefully

If, after deploying these responses, the buyer still doesn't see the need — and you genuinely believe they don't have the problem — qualify out with professionalism:

"Based on our conversation, it sounds like [your solution area] isn't a priority for you right now, and your current setup is meeting your needs. I don't want to waste your time pushing something that doesn't fit. Here's what I'd suggest: I'll check back in [6 months / Q4 / when a specific event happens] to see if anything has changed. In the meantime, I'll send you [one piece of genuinely useful content — a market report, a benchmark study] that might be valuable regardless of whether we work together.

If anything changes on your end before then, you've got my number. Sound good?"

**Why this matters:** A graceful exit preserves the relationship for the future. The #1 source of future deals is "they called me back when the situation changed." The #1 way to ensure they NEVER call back is to push past a genuine "no."
