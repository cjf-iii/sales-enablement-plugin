# Authority Objection Playbook

## Common Forms

- "I need to run this by my boss"
- "This isn't my decision alone"
- "I'll need to get approval from [name/title]"
- "Our committee needs to review this"
- "Let me take this back to the team"
- "My agency handles these decisions"

## Core Principle

Never try to go around your contact to reach the decision-maker. It destroys trust and kills champions. Instead, EQUIP your contact to sell internally. Your job is to make them successful in the conversations you're not in.

---

## Response 1: Champion Coaching

**When to use:** Your contact is willing to advocate for you but doesn't know how to present the case to their leadership. This is the most common authority scenario.

**The response:**

"Absolutely — that's how good organizations make decisions. I want to help make that conversation as easy as possible for you. A couple of questions so I can prepare the right materials:

1. What does [decision-maker name/title] care about most? Is it ROI, competitive positioning, risk, operational efficiency, or something else?
2. What objections do you think they'll raise? What would make them say no?
3. What format would work best — would they prefer a 1-page executive brief they can scan in 2 minutes, or a more detailed proposal?

Based on your answers, I'll put together something specifically designed for that conversation. I'll also walk you through the key talking points so you feel completely prepared. Would it be helpful if we did a 15-minute practice run where I play your boss and you pitch it? That way we can anticipate tough questions before they come up."

**Why it works:** You're positioning yourself as a partner, not a bypasser. You're making your contact look prepared and competent in front of their boss. You're also getting critical intelligence about the economic buyer's priorities and objections — which you need to win the deal regardless.

**Follow-up:** After coaching, send the champion a pre-written email they can customize and forward. Include: 1-page exec brief, 3 key talking points, and the ROI headline. Make it easy for them to act immediately.

---

## Response 2: Executive Brief

**When to use:** The decision-maker needs information but won't take a meeting with a vendor. Your contact is the conduit.

**The response:**

"That makes sense. Let me put together a one-page executive brief that's designed specifically for someone at [decision-maker's] level. It won't be a sales pitch — it'll be a business case. Here's what I'll include:

- The problem in their language: [how it connects to their strategic priorities]
- The cost of not solving it: $[quantified impact]
- The proposed solution in 3 bullets
- ROI and payback period
- One relevant case study (same industry)
- Clear next step

I'll have it to you by [tomorrow/specific date]. Would it be helpful if I also included 2-3 questions your boss might ask, with suggested answers? That way you're armed for anything."

**Why it works:** C-level executives won't read a 10-page proposal from a vendor they've never met. A 1-pager that respects their time and speaks their language has a much higher chance of being read — and of generating a positive response.

---

## Response 3: Upward Introduction

**When to use:** The deal is significant enough to warrant an executive-to-executive conversation, and your contact is open to facilitating it.

**The response:**

"I appreciate that this needs leadership buy-in. Given the size and strategic nature of this initiative, would it be helpful if we facilitated an executive-to-executive conversation? Our [VP/SVP/CRO — someone at peer level to their decision-maker] would be happy to have a brief call with [their EB name] — not a sales pitch, but a strategic conversation about how companies in your industry are approaching [their challenge].

This can actually make the process easier for you — it takes the weight off you being the sole advocate, and it gives [EB name] a peer-level perspective on why this matters.

How would you feel about that? I don't want to overstep — if you'd prefer to handle it internally, I'll make sure you have everything you need to be successful."

**Why it works:** Executive-to-executive conversations accelerate deals because: (1) it signals that both organizations take the partnership seriously, (2) it bypasses the "vendor pitch" dynamic, (3) it gives the EB a forum to ask questions they wouldn't ask a mid-level contact, and (4) it demonstrates your organization's investment in the relationship.

**Important:** Always ask permission before reaching up. Going over your contact's head without consent is a relationship-ender.

## Handling "My Agency Handles This"

This is a media/advertising-specific authority objection. The agency isn't the economic buyer — but they often control the evaluation.

**Strategy: Position as a partner to the agency, not a threat.**

"I appreciate that — agencies bring valuable perspective and execution capability. Here's what I'd suggest: rather than positioning this as something that bypasses your agency, what if we presented this jointly? We've found that the most productive evaluations happen when the brand, the agency, and the solution provider are all in the room together.

Your agency wants the best tools and inventory for your campaigns — that's their job. And we can actually make their job easier by [specific value to the agency — better data for planning, more efficient buying, stronger campaign performance they can take credit for].

Would you be open to a three-way meeting? I'll let you drive the agenda, and I'll position this as a resource for your agency, not a replacement. Most agencies respond well to that framing."

---

## Red Flags: Authority as a Polite Brush-Off

Sometimes "I need to check with my boss" means "I'm not interested but I don't want to tell you no directly." Watch for:

- They say they'll "discuss internally" but won't commit to a follow-up date
- They don't want to share the decision-maker's name or criteria
- They resist any offer to help prepare materials
- They've used this objection in two or more consecutive conversations

**If you suspect a brush-off:** Ask directly but gently: "I want to be respectful of everyone's time. If you were to take this to your team, how do you think they'd react? Is this something your organization genuinely wants to pursue, or are there reservations we should discuss?"

The honesty question often unlocks the real objection — which you can then address directly.
